Know the 90°F fan rule during extreme heat

A fan can feel cooling, but when the room itself is hotter than 90°F (32°C), relying on a fan alone can increase body temperature. Move to air conditioning or a public cooling location instead.

Check the indoor temperature: Put a simple thermometer in the room where you spend the most time. Do not judge safety only by how the moving air feels.
Below 90°F indoors: A fan may help, especially with light clothing, hydration, closed blinds, and reduced use of ovens or other heat-producing appliances.
At or above 90°F indoors: Use air conditioning if available, or go to an air-conditioned library, shopping center, community facility, or local cooling center. Call 2-1-1 or contact local public health or emergency management for cooling locations.
Plan outdoor activity: Move exercise, yard work, errands, and physically demanding tasks to the coolest part of the day. Take more frequent breaks in shade or air conditioning and drink fluids regularly rather than waiting for thirst.
Check on others: Older adults, infants and children, pregnant people, outdoor workers, and people with heart, lung, kidney, or other chronic conditions can become ill sooner. Check on anyone living alone.
Medicines and heat: Some medicines can affect hydration or temperature control, and some must be protected from high temperatures. Do not stop or change medicine without professional advice; ask a pharmacist or clinician how to store and manage it during heat.
Confusion, fainting, seizures, or very high body temperature may mean heat stroke. Call 911 immediately, move the person to a cooler place, remove outer clothing, and cool with water, cold compresses, or ice while help is coming.
