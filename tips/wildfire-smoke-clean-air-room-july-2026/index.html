Make one cleaner-air room before smoke gets bad

With wildfire smoke affecting large parts of the United States, the most useful household action is to prepare one room where outdoor smoke is kept as low as possible.

Check current air quality: Use AirNow’s Fire and Smoke Map or your local air-quality alert before outdoor exercise, errands, or children’s activities. Smoke levels can change during the day.
Choose one room: Pick a room where people spend a lot of time. Keep its doors and windows closed, and avoid smoking, frying food, burning candles, or vacuuming there while smoke is heavy.
Filter the air: Run a correctly sized portable air cleaner as much as possible, preferably at a high setting. If you use central HVAC, set it to recirculate and use a MERV-13 filter or the highest-efficiency filter your system can safely handle.
Reduce outdoor exposure: Move strenuous activity indoors or postpone it when air quality is poor. If you must be outside in smoke, use a well-fitting NIOSH-approved N95 or P100 respirator; cloth masks do not filter wildfire smoke effectively.
Protect higher-risk people: Children, pregnant people, adults 65 and older, and people with asthma, heart disease, or other chronic conditions may be affected sooner. Keep medicines available and follow the person’s health-care plan.
Important heat trade-off: Do not keep windows closed in a dangerously hot home without adequate cooling. EPA guidance says to seek a cooler, cleaner-air location when you cannot stay safely cool indoors.
Leave immediately if local officials order an evacuation. A cleaner-air room reduces smoke exposure; it is not protection from a nearby fire.
